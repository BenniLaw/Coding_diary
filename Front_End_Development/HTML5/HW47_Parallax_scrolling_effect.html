In statistics, σ (sigma) represents the true standard deviation of a population. It describes the real spread or variability of all data points in the entire group we care about — for example, the heights of all people in a country. However, in most real-world situations we never have access to every single member of a population. Because of this, σ is typically unknown.

Since we cannot directly observe σ, we rely on s, the sample standard deviation. The value s is calculated from the data we actually collect — such as the heights of 20 randomly chosen people. In essence, s is our best estimate of σ, based only on the limited evidence we have in our sample. The more data we gather, the closer s tends to get to the true σ.

When the sample size is large, the estimation error between s and σ becomes very small, and the normal (Z) distribution works well for constructing confidence intervals. However, when the sample size is small, using s instead of σ introduces more uncertainty. To account for this extra uncertainty, statisticians use the t-distribution, which has fatter tails and produces slightly wider confidence intervals.
